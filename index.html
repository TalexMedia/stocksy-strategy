02 The Equity You've Built

Three Strengths Already In Place

All three are earned, all three are working. Any new video would build on what's already here.

Community equity. Stocksy is owned by more than 1,800 artists across 80 countries, runs on one member and one vote, and has paid out over $50 million to the people who own it, against an industry norm of roughly 20 percent royalties. That is the most distinctive cast in the category and the exact subject the whole industry is now arguing about: fair pay for the humans who make the work.

Voice equity. Stocksy already says what it stands for, plainly. The Instagram line is "Created and curated by humans." The editorial brand is "Stocksy Ideas: Stories for meaningful work." The CEO's first public thesis was that the collection "isn't just about visuals; it's about storytelling." In a category flooded with machine-made images, that point of view is already written down and consistent.

Production equity. Stocksy already makes video, and makes it regularly: the interview series, the five-part Trends breakdowns, brand films, and live events in NYC. The team already shoots, edits, and publishes on a steady cadence, and the channel has already shown one of those pieces can reach 80,000 people when it leans into the right theme.

The one thing the public presence does not have yet is a returning series that puts the artists and the value of human-made work at its center, the way the Vincent Wanga interview did once. That is a format gap, not a quality gap, and it is the kind of work Stocksy already curates for everyone else, now made into a series its own community would want to watch.

03 Lead With Value

The Legendeering Framework, In One Page

The methodology behind every Talex production. Five directives, one core principle.

Legendeering is Talex's proprietary strategy. The core principle is straightforward: lead with value, always. The brand earns trust by giving without expectation, and that trust grows over time because people keep choosing to watch.

Five directives drive it:

  • ·Give Without Expectation. Value delivered without strings. The kind of generosity that builds loyalty money cannot buy.
  • ·Be Relentlessly Authentic. Say true things. Show real work. Speak like a human being.
  • ·It's Not About You. It's About Them. The audience has to come first. It's all about their wants and needs and making something they will choose to watch.
  • ·Start With Story, Follow With Facts. Story gives information shape, context, and emotional weight. Facts alone rarely move people.
  • ·Consistency Is Key. Trust is built in moments, repeated over time.

Stocksy is the expert at curating human-made visual media and paying the artists who make it. Talex would add the long-form video: documentary films built around the artists, the mission, and the value of human work the collection already stands for. The three ideas below are what that looks like for a cooperative at your scale.

04 Three Concepts For Stocksy

Three Long-Form Concepts For The Stocksy Brand

Each one builds on equity Stocksy has already earned, and each is something only Stocksy could make.

01
"The Owners"
This is a returning profile series in the spirit of Netflix's Chef's Table, which gives one master a full episode, slow and reverent, until you understand the person and the craft together. Here the subject is one Stocksy artist-owner per episode. We meet a photographer or filmmaker trying to make an independent living in an industry that historically paid creators a fraction of each sale and is now flooded with machine-made images. The stakes are their livelihood and their creative independence. Stocksy enters the story as the cooperative that pays them 50 to 75 percent and shares its profit, with 1,800 owners behind them. The episode ends with their work inside a Fortune 500 campaign, in their own words. Trace is the recurring guide who frames why this artist, and this model, matters.
02
"Made By Humans"
This is a returning explainer series in the spirit of Netflix's Abstract: The Art of Design, which builds each episode around one person and the single question that drives their work, until the audience understands why it matters. The question here is the one Stocksy already proved travels at 80,000 views: what can human-made visual work do that a generated image cannot? Each episode follows one brand or agency creative director under pressure to fill a campaign with cheap, generic AI imagery, only to watch it stop connecting with the people it is meant to reach. Their brand's credibility is on the line. Stocksy enters with the curated human-made collection and the artist behind the chosen frame. The episode ends with the creative director explaining, on camera, the campaign that finally landed because it was real.
03
"The Co-op"
This is a returning, fly-on-the-wall documentary in the spirit of Welcome to Wrexham and Drive to Survive, which put cameras inside an organization most people never get to see and made the audience care about the people running it. Each season follows Stocksy through one real decision that tests whether it can stay an artist-owned alternative while the rest of the industry consolidates and automates. Trace and co-founder Bruce Livingstone carry the story, alongside the artist-owners whose livelihoods depend on the outcome. We stay in the room as the call gets made, and the season ends with what it cost and what it protected. The one-off "We Are United" film already proved the appetite. This is the version that comes back.
